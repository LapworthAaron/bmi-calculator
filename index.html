What is BMI?

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a simple numerical measure that uses your height and weight to estimate whether your body weight falls within a healthy range. It is widely used by healthcare professionals, including the NHS, WHO, and CDC, as a quick screening tool for potential weight-related health risks.

While BMI does not directly measure body fat, it correlates well enough with more precise methods to serve as a useful starting point. A healthy BMI generally falls between 18.5 and 24.9. Values outside this range may indicate increased risk for conditions such as heart disease, diabetes, and other chronic illnesses.

ℹ️ Limitations of BMI ▼

BMI is a useful population-level screening tool but has well-documented limitations:

  • Athletes & muscular people — may show a high BMI despite having low body fat, because muscle is denser than fat.
  • Older adults — may have a healthy BMI but carry excess fat due to age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia).
  • Ethnicity — South Asian, East Asian, Middle Eastern, and Black African populations face higher metabolic risk at lower BMI thresholds.
  • Children & teenagers — BMI should only be interpreted using age- and gender-specific percentile charts (not adult ranges).
  • Pregnancy — BMI is not an appropriate measure during pregnancy.
  • Fat distribution — BMI does not account for where fat is stored. Abdominal (visceral) fat is more harmful than fat elsewhere; waist circumference is a useful complementary measure.
🧪 How BMI is Calculated ▼

Metric formula: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m²)

Imperial formula: BMI = (weight (lbs) × 703) ÷ height² (in²)

BMI Prime is your BMI divided by 25 (the upper limit of healthy). A value of exactly 1.0 means you are at the top of the healthy range. Values below 1.0 are healthy; above 1.0 indicates overweight or obese.

Ponderal Index (PI) = weight (kg) ÷ height³ (m³). An alternative to BMI that accounts for body shape differences, especially for very tall or short individuals. A healthy PI is typically 11–14 kg/m³.

BMI Percentiles for Children (ages 2–19)

For children and teens, BMI is calculated the same way as for adults, but the result is interpreted differently. Because body composition changes with age and differs between boys and girls, a child’s BMI is compared against age- and sex-specific reference data from the CDC growth charts.

The result is expressed as a percentile — for example, a child at the 60th percentile has a BMI greater than 60% of children of the same age and sex. The CDC categories are:

  • Underweight: below the 5th percentile
  • Healthy weight: 5th to below the 85th percentile
  • Overweight: 85th to below the 95th percentile
  • Obese: 95th percentile or above
